In-class Exercises

Part 1: Combining Geoprocessing Operations

  1. Download today's data
  2. Open the PaCounty2015_01 and DCNR_StateParks201308 shapefiles.
  3. Open the CSV Oil_Gas_Well_Historical_Production_Report.
  4. Reproject the wells layer. Use State Plane Pennsylvania North (3650).
  5. Buffer the wells by two miles and dissolve the results.
  6. Use Select by location to determine how many state parks are within the buffered area around the wells.
  7. Intersect the counties and the wells buffer to find the parts of counties that are within two miles of a well.
  8. Calculate the area of the intersected features. This is the area of each county within two miles of a well.
  9. Join the area from the previous step to the original counties layer.
  10. Style the counties by the percentage of area within two miles of a well.
  11. If you have time, use a similar process to the one above to style the counties by percentage of area within the Marcellus Shale (Limit_Marcellus_Poly_110317). You wouldn't need to buffer the shale in this case.

Part 2: Model builder

  1. Make a model with the graphical modeler (Processing > Graphical Modeler) that:
    1. takes an input vector layer,
    2. reprojects it into 2263, and
    3. buffers it by 2000 feet.
  2. Test the model with Public_School_Locations, included in today's data.
  3. If you have time, add another input (for example, a number or another layer) and another algorithm to your model.

Part 3: Hexbins

Use hexbins to look at the density of uber_2014_04_01 or Graffiti_Locations.

  1. Open the points file of your choosing (uber_2014_04_01 or Graffiti_Locations).
  2. Move your project into a projection where you know the units (eg, 2263 is in feet).
  3. Use the QGIS search bar to search for Create grid.
  4. Select Hexagon for the grid type, set the grid extent to Use layer/canvas extent > Use extent from (your layer name).
  5. Set the Horizontal spacing and Vertical spacing to 1000 feet to start.
  6. Pick a suitable place to save to and create the grid.
  7. If the grid's hexagons are too large or too small, repeat the process but change the Horizontal spacing or Vertical spacing. These determine the height and width of the hexagons.
  8. Count the number of points in each hexagon and make a choropleth with the resulting layer.
